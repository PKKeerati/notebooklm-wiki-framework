# Benchmarking MLIPs for Mg-H₂ Phase Transitions: PCI Curves, Dopants, and Grand Canonical Ensembles

## Key Findings

> [!important] Plateau pressures define the thermodynamic window for Mg-H₂ hydrogen storage
> Experimental [[PCI_curves]] for the $Mg + H_2 \rightarrow MgH_2$ reaction exhibit plateau pressures of **0.1–1 bar for absorption** and **0.01–0.1 bar for desorption** at 300–400°C, establishing the operational range for hydrogen storage applications. The observed hysteresis ($P_{abs}/P_{des} \approx 10$–100) arises from kinetic barriers such as hydride nucleation and lattice strain, with particle size reduction (<50 nm) and surface oxides exacerbating the effect. These pressures are validated by experimental PCI measurements in [[Q1]], serving as critical benchmarks for MLIP validation. The hysteresis ratio is particularly sensitive to material processing (e.g., ball-milling, catalyst addition), underscoring the need for MLIPs to capture non-equilibrium pathways.

> [!important] DFT systematically overestimates Mg-H₂ plateau pressures by 2–3×
> Density functional theory (DFT) calculations using functionals like PBE, PBE-D3, and rev-vdW-DF2 predict plateau pressures **2–3× higher** than experimental values for $MgH_2$ formation. This discrepancy stems from DFT’s incomplete treatment of van der Waals interactions and anharmonic lattice vibrations, which stabilize the hydride phase. The overestimation is consistent across multiple studies ([[Q2]], [[Q5]]) and persists even with dispersion-corrected functionals. This limitation restricts DFT’s utility as a sole reference for training MLIPs, necessitating hybrid approaches that incorporate experimental data or higher-level methods (e.g., quantum Monte Carlo) for accurate energetics.

> [!important] Hysteresis in Mg-H₂ phase transitions originates from kinetic barriers, not DFT-predicted equilibrium
> Experimental PCI curves reveal **hysteresis ratios ($P_{abs}/P_{des}$) of 10–100**, while DFT predicts negligible hysteresis due to its equilibrium assumptions. The discrepancy arises from kinetic barriers such as hydride nucleation, lattice expansion, and defect formation, which are not captured by DFT’s harmonic approximations. Hysteresis is amplified in nanoscale Mg particles (<50 nm) and surface-oxidized samples, as shown in [[Q1]] and [[Q5]]. This highlights the need for MLIPs to explicitly model non-equilibrium pathways, such as those encountered during ball-milling or high-pressure cycling, to accurately predict real-world hydrogen storage performance.

> [!important] Dopants reduce Mg-H₂ desorption enthalpy by 20–30 kJ/mol H₂ via catalytic and strain mechanisms
> Dopants such as Ni and Ga lower the desorption enthalpy of $MgH_2$ from ~74–80 kJ/mol $H_2$ to ~50–60 kJ/mol $H_2$, as quantified by PCI curves and temperature-programmed desorption (TPD) experiments ([[Q4]]). Ni catalyzes $H_2$ dissociation by tuning the d-band center, while Ga induces lattice strain that weakens Mg-H bonds. The magnitude of this effect correlates with dopant electronegativity (e.g., Ni: 1.91 vs. Ga: 1.81), suggesting a descriptor-based approach for MLIPs could capture these mechanisms. This reduction in enthalpy is critical for enabling hydrogen release at lower temperatures, a key requirement for practical storage systems.

> [!important] SOAP-GAP and ACE MLIPs outperform DFT in Mg-H₂ energetics but require large training datasets
> [[SOAP-GAP]] and [[Atomic_Cluster_Expansion_(ACE)]] MLIPs improve upon DFT’s overestimation of plateau pressures by explicitly capturing multi-body interactions in Mg-H systems. SOAP-GAP encodes local atomic environments using spherical harmonics, while ACE leverages polynomial expansions to represent atomic clusters. Benchmarking studies ([[Q3]]) demonstrate their superior accuracy for Mg-H energetics, but their performance hinges on the size and diversity of training datasets. Both methods require thousands of ab initio configurations to achieve convergence, posing challenges for high-throughput applications. Their success underscores the need for curated datasets at PCI-relevant conditions.

> [!warning] Equivariant transformers show promise for symmetry preservation but face computational trade-offs
> [[Equivariant_transformers]] preserve rotational and permutational symmetries in Mg-H systems, improving the accuracy of phase transition predictions by reducing the need for data augmentation. Early results suggest they can reproduce PCI curve features more faithfully than traditional MLIPs, but their computational cost remains prohibitive for large-scale simulations. The trade-off between accuracy and efficiency may limit their adoption for industrial applications, where rapid screening of dopants or processing conditions is prioritized. Further optimization (e.g., sparse attention mechanisms) could mitigate these limitations.

> [!warning] μNPT ensembles may outperform NPT for Mg-H phase coexistence but lack validation
> Grand canonical Monte Carlo ([[muNPT]]) explicitly models hydrogen chemical potential, offering a theoretical advantage over NPT MD for capturing phase coexistence in Mg-H systems. While μNPT has shown success in other materials (e.g., MOFs), no direct comparisons exist for Mg-H₂. The lack of validated descriptors (e.g., [[SOAP]], [[ACSF]]) for hydrogen’s light mass and quantum effects may hinder its accuracy. A systematic comparison of μNPT vs. NPT for Mg-H phase transitions is needed to determine whether the added complexity of μNPT justifies its use for PCI curve prediction.

> [!important] Descriptor transferability is limited for hydrogen due to quantum effects and high mobility
> Descriptors such as [[SOAP]] and [[ACSF]] exhibit moderate transferability to Mg-H systems but struggle with hydrogen’s unique properties, including its light mass, high mobility, and zero-point energy. SOAP’s spherical harmonics and ACSF’s geometric features are less effective for hydrogen, leading to inaccuracies in μNPT simulations of phase coexistence ([[Q6]]). This limitation is particularly problematic for PCI curve prediction, where hydrogen’s behavior drives the phase transition. Developing hydrogen-specific descriptors or incorporating quantum corrections (e.g., path integral MD) may be necessary to improve accuracy.

> [!important] Dopant-aware MLIPs are absent, limiting predictive power for industrial applications
> Current MLIPs treat dopants (e.g., Ni, Ga) as generic atoms, ignoring their catalytic effects on $H_2$ dissociation and lattice strain. This gap explains their poor performance for doped Mg-H PCI curves, as they cannot capture dopant-specific mechanisms such as d-band center tuning or vacancy formation. Incorporating dopant-aware descriptors (e.g., electronegativity, d-band centers, local strain fields) into MLIPs is essential for modeling industrial hydrogen storage materials, where dopants are routinely used to tune kinetics and thermodynamics.

> [!important] Surface-mediated phase transitions exhibit faster kinetics than bulk transitions
> Surface-mediated Mg-H phase transitions occur more rapidly than bulk transitions due to reduced coordination numbers and the presence of surface oxides, which lower nucleation barriers. Experimental PCI curves for nanoscale Mg particles (<50 nm) show distinct plateau pressures and reduced hysteresis compared to bulk samples ([[Q7]]). However, no MLIPs currently differentiate between surface and bulk effects, limiting their applicability to real-world materials where surface-to-volume ratios are high. Addressing this gap requires surface-specific training data and descriptors that encode local coordination environments.

> [!important] Non-equilibrium pathways are unmodeled, restricting MLIP applicability to real-world conditions
> No MLIPs or descriptors address non-equilibrium Mg-H phase transitions encountered in industrial processes such as ball-milling or high-pressure cycling. Current models assume equilibrium conditions, failing to capture dynamic effects like defect formation, amorphization, or temperature gradients. This gap limits their utility for predicting hydrogen storage performance under realistic operating conditions. Incorporating non-equilibrium descriptors (e.g., local strain rates, defect concentrations) or active learning strategies could bridge this divide.
